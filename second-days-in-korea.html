Second Days in Korea

erikburk 9 March 2026

Whats up team it has now been almost four weeks since I landed here and I have been settling in and everything. My courses are starting to move past repetition, I went on a trip to Busan, and I have bought a bike. Many a thing happening.

Busan Trip

It was a cool city. I honestly liked Seoul a lot more, felt easier to get around in and just generally less cars etc. Unpopular opinion maybe but yeah. There was some really good second hand shopping in the markets in Busan though. We also went out clubbing the first night - really cool place. We actually got rejected for being foreigners at the first place though, so that was a totally new experience.

Day two was the aforementioned shopping and going to the spa time. I will talk more about that later.

Day three was Buddha's birthday, which we were totally unprepared for. We went to a really cool temple on the coast, along with seemingly every single buddhist in East Asia. It was a cool temple but way to many people. I guess us coming and touristing did not help the fact. The day was finished off with a trip to Busan's IKEA. I can confirm that they have meatballs. However, no lingondricka or flädersaft. You can't win every fight I guess.

Erik discovering Korean Spas

Alright so this was something recommended by Madeline before I went, but man this is just amazing. It's both a spa part and a relax part, and in total costs like 18 000 KRW (=100SEK ish). You enter, undress completely and just sit in hot baths butt naked with some old korean men. You don't understand them and they don't understand you. It's great. I was at one in Pohang first, and also at a much larger one in Busan. And honestly, I liked the Pohang one more. Busan just felt too big and was more just a bathhouse with kids running around everywhere. Nothing for real Korean intellectuals like myself.

In the relax area, theres like places to take a nap with different temperatures, massage chairs and (sometimes) actual massage places. You can also get snacks and noodles while inside. And craziest thing - you can just stay the night and sleep in the spa if you feel like it ??!! Top ten crazy things ever. When I go on my bike trip to Seoul that will definitely totally happen I could feasibly just find a spa and chill for a couple of hours and then go to sleep in the same place, for no extra cost. Absolute bonkers. Anyways I know what business I will be starting when I come back to Sweden. Who cares about engineering physics?

The very real bike trip that I will totally do // Buying a bike second-hand

A case study on Korean culture and why old Korean men scare me sometimes

One of my large goals when in Montreal was to bike across the border south, and continue down into New York. I calculated that it should take less than a week to do. Anyways, due to unforeseen circumstances, I am no longer in Montreal and cannot finish that goal. Instead my goal is now to do a similar thing in Korea - I'll cross the border to the socialist brothers in the north and hang out in Pyeongyang for a couple of days. I heard they love biking up there.

In case that goal doesn't work out either, I guess Seoul can suffice. There's a great cross-country bike road that is more or less paved the entire way. My plan is to take the bus to Daegu and bike the remaining 450 km up to Seoul in about 3.5 days. Will I make it all the way in that amount of time? Possibly, I honestly do not know. We'll see. But the first step in any bikepacker's dream is browsing reddit for advice buying a bike. My Korean friend helped me on the second hand site and everything. We found a nice looking bike, and contacted the guy. Turns out - he wasn't home at the time, but since the bike stood outside with a codelock, he offered to sell it anyways through a money transfer. My #1 king Changhee pulled through yet again and helped me with the korean bank transfer so that I could get my hands on the bike. I am pretty sure it's a carbon fiber frame, which is absolutely crazy for the 1400SEK price that I paid. 

Anyways, transaction is complete and I walk away with my new bike in hand. And here is where my very interesting cultural observations come:

  • I had to pump the tyres, so I found the nearest bike shop still open on a sunday night. The guy there doesn't speak any english, and I obviously do not speak any Korean, but I could signal to him that I needed a pump. He follows me outside and actually like starts yelling angrily at me in Korean because I cannot comprehend how his silly pump contraption works. I still do not understand if I did something wrong or not, but he just seemed pissed to have a foreigner in his shop. Just like refuse me service or something instead then? Cmon gramps
  • A lot of things in Korea are very clean in the moment, but veeeeery poorly maintained and upkept. Many facades could use a lift for example. Streets and sidewalks are another example. There were so many cracks and potholes that I felt motionsick for a part of the bike trip. Hopefully the cross-country road is better upkept atleast. But the priorities are interesting coming from a country focusing more or less on the direct opposite - very few places in Sweden look genuinely run-down, even in lower-income areas, but trash on the street and general cleanliness is way lower than here.
  • bro just move your ass youre walking four ppl in teh bike lane looking straight at me while the pedestrian lane is litteraly 2m to your right just let me bike past genuinely what is wrong with you
  • there were genuinely multiple times where I was close to running into someone because people just don't have any sense of their surroundings here. I am not the only cyclist in Pohang by any stretch, but they just couldn't for the life of me move out of the damm way.

Anyways that was all my cultural observations for this week. Stay tuned for next week's foreigner unfriendliness observations in this fantastic country.
